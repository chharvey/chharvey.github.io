Classroom Assessment Tools

Updated April 01, 2012.

A summary of the most common types of classroom assessment tools.
Item Type Properties & Uses Advantages Limitations Suggestions for Construction Evaluation
Objective Test Items Short-Answer Questions
  • Wide variety of relatively simple learning outcomes; recall of memorized information
    • Except in STEM: supply type is superior to selection type
  • May measure complex achievement in problem-solving items
  • Easiest to construct
  • Student must supply answer; reduces guessing
  • Partial knowledge is often insufficient for correctness
  • Unsuitability for measuring complex learning outcomes
  • Difficult to score due to possible ambiguous answers
    • Except in STEM
  • Word the item so that required answer is brief and specific.
  • Do not take statements directly from textbook.
  • Direct questions are more desirable than incomplete statements.
  • If the answer is to be expressed in units, indicate the type desired.
  • Answer blanks should be the same length and vertically aligned.
  • Don't include too many blanks.
 see p. 349
True-False Questions
  • Two possible opposing answers
  • Measures ability to verify statements of fact, definitions, principles
  • May measure complex achievement: if testing ability to recognize cause-and-effect
  • Easily constructed
  • Efficiently and quickly answered
  • Wide sampling of material
  • Not particularly useful beyond knowledge
  • Susceptibility to guessing
  • Students are not required to know correct answer on False statements
  • Avoid broad general statements if they are to be judged true or false.
  • Avoid trivial statements.
  • Avoid the use of negative statements.
  • Avoid long, complex sentences.
  • Avoid including two ideas in one statement, except for cause-and-effect.
  • If opinion is used, attribute it to a source.
  • True and false statements should be about equal in length.
  • The number of true and false statements should be about equal.
Matching Exercises
  • A set of premises corresponds to a set of responses
  • Not necessarily pigeonholed
  • Premises and responses are each homogeneous
  • Measures factual information based on simple associations
  • Compact form allows to measure a large body of material in a short time
  • Easily constructed
  • Measurement of factual information
  • Hard to find homogeneous material
  • Use only homogeneous material in a single matching exercise.
  • Include an unequal number of responses and premises; instruct that responses may be used once, more than once, or not at all.
  • Keep list of items to be matched brief and place shorter responses on the right.
  • Arrange the list of responses in logical order (alphabetical, numerical, etc).
  • Indicate in the directions the basis for matching.
  • Place entire matching exercise on the same page.
Multiple-Choice Questions
  • Can measure simple to complex learning outcomes
  • Adaptable to most subject-matter content
  • Students must choose correct answer; more than identify as incorrect
  • Greater reliability than T/F
  • No need for homogeneity
  • Amenable to diagnosis
  • Limited to the verbal level
  • Not well adapted to measuring problem-solving, organization, or presentation of ideas
  • Difficult to find sufficient number of incorrect, yet plausible distractors
  • The stem of the item should be meaningful by itself, present definite problem.
  • The stem should include much of the item as possible, free of irrelevant material.
  • Use negatively stated stem only when significant learning outcomes require it.
  • All alternatives should be grammatically consistent with stem.
  • Item should contain only one correct or clearly best answer.
  • Items used to measure understanding should contain some novelty, but not too much.
  • All distractors should be plausible.
  • Verbal associations between stem and correct answer should be avoided.
  • Relative length of alternatives should not provide clues.
  • Correct answer should appear in evenly but randomly distributed positions.
  • Use inclusive/exclusive alternatives sparingly.
  • Don't use multiple-choice when other item types are more appropriate.
Interpretive Exercises
  • May measure a variety of complex learning outcomes
  • All students confronted with same task
  • Control amount of factual information given
  • Measures ability to interpret prose, charts, graphs, maps, and other communication media
  • Measures more complex learning outcomes than single objective item
  • Obtains greater depth, breadth of skills by grouping related items
  • Minimize influence of irrelevant facts
  • Difficult to construct
  • Demand specific behaviors
  • Heavy demand of reading skill
  • Cannot measure overall approach to problem solving
  • Confined to recognition level
  • Select introductory material that is:
    • relevant to objectives of the course.
    • appropriate to studentsâ€™ curricular experience, reading level.
    • new to students.
    • brief but meaningful.
  • Revise introductory material for clarity, conciseness, greater interpretive value.
  • Construct test items that require analysis and interpretation of introductory material.
  • Make number of test items roughly proportional to length of introductory material.
  • In constructing test items, see suggestions for Objective Items.
  • In constructing key-type items,
    • make categories homogeneous and mutually exclusive.
    • develop standard key categories where applicable.
Performance Assessments Essay Questions
  • Freedom of response
  • Used to measure learning outcomes not readily measured by objective items
  • Evaluate ability to conceptualize, construct, organize, integrate, relate, evaluate, etc.
  • Evaluate how well students communicate ideas
  • Evaluate student writing abilities
  • Measures complex learning outcomes inassessable by other means
    • only if constructed as carefully as objective test items
  • Emphasis on integration and application of thinking and problem-solving skills
  • Enable direct evaluation of writing skills
  • Ease of construction
  • Contribution to student learning: the process of formulating a response can be a learning exercise
  • Unreliability of scoring
    • Requires adequate attention to learning outcomes and scoring rubrics
  • Large amount of time required for scoring
  • Limited sampling of content provided
  • Restirct the use of essay questions to those learning outcomes that cannot be measured satisfactorily by objective items.
  • Construct questions that will call fourth the skills specified in the learning standards.
  • Phrase the question so that the student's task is clearly defined.
  • Indicate an approximate time limit for each question.
  • Avoid the use of optional questions.
  • Restricted-Response: teacher writes example of expected response
  • Extended-Response:
    • Analytic Scoring Rubrics: see p. 249
    • Holistic Scoring Rubrics: see p. 251
  • Prepare an outline of the expected answer in advance.
  • Use the scoring rubic that is most appropriate.
  • Decide how to handle factors that are irrelevant to the learning outcomes measured.
  • Evaluate all responses to one question before going on to the next one.
  • Evaluate the answers without looking at the students' names.
  • If especially important decisions are to be based on the results, obtain two or more independent ratings.
Other Performance-Based Assessments
  • Best suited for applications with less-structured problems
  • Restricted Response: typically start with MCQ or SAQ, then extended by asking for further explanation
  • Extended Response: may require information from beyond the task itself ('Net, Library, survey, etc.), then assembled into a product
  • Advantages and limitations parallel with Essay Questions.
  • Clearly communicate instructional goals that involve complex performances
  • Measure complex learning outcomes inassessable by other means
  • Provide a means of assessing process and product
  • Implement approaches that are suggested by modern learning theory
  • Unreliability of performance ratings across teachers; time
  • Time-consuming
  • Focus on learning outcomes that require complex cognitive skills and student performances.
  • Select or develop tasks that represent both the content and the skills that are central to important learning outcomes.
  • Minimize the dependence of task performance on skills that are irrelevant to the intended purpose of the assignment task.
  • Provide the necessary scaffolding for students to be able to understand the task and what is expected.
  • Construct task directions so that the student's task is clearly understood.
  • Clearly communicate performance expectations in terms of the scoring rubrics by which the performances will be judged.
  • Characteristics should be educationally significant.
  • Identify the learning outcomes that the task is intended to assess.
  • Characteristics should be directly observable.
  • Characteristics and points on the scale should be clearly defined.
  • Select the type of scoring rubric that is most appropriate for the task and the purpose of the assessment.
  • Between 3 and 7 rating positions should be provided.
  • Rate performances of all students on one task before going on to the next one.
  • Rate performances without knowledge of the students' names.
  • When results from a performance assessment are likely to have long-term consequences for students, ratings from several observers should be combined.
Portfolios
  • A purposeful collection of pieces of student work to meet a particular goal
  • Readily integrated with instruction
  • Provide the chance for students to show off their own work
  • Encourage students to become reflective learners and develop self-evaluation skills
  • Provide parents with concrete examples of students' work
  • Provide focus for parent-student-teacher discussion and conferences
  • Time-consuming to assemble
  • Time-consuming to evaluate
  • Ratings have relatively low reliability
  • Misconception that they can be easily created
  • Misconception of portfolio vs. folder containing student's work

Observational Techniques Anecdotal Records
  • Factual descriptions of meaningful events observed in class
  • Obtain data pertinent to a variety of learning outcomes, and to many aspects of personal and social development
  • Observations are:
    • confined to areas of behavior inassessable by other means
    • limited to only a few types of behavior at any given time
    • restricted to those few students who are most in need of help
  • Depict actual behavior in natural situations
  • Facilitate gathering evidence on events that are exceptional but significant
  • Can be used with very young students / students with limited communication skills
  • Maintaining a system of records costs time
  • Difficulty of being objective when observing and reporting behavior
  • Obtaining an adequate sample of behavior
  • Determine in advance what to observe, but be alert for unusual behavior.
  • Analyze observational records for possible sources of bias.
  • Observe and record enough of the situation to make the behavior meaningful.
  • Make a record of the incident as soon after the observation as possible.
  • Limit each anecdote to a brief description of a single incident.
  • Keep the factual description of the incident and your interpretation of it separate.
  • Record both positive and negative behavioral incidents.
  • Collect a number of anecdotes on a student before drawing inferences concerning typical behavior.
  • Obtain practice in writing anecdotal records.

Peer Appraisal
  • Administered in a short amount of time to all age levels
  • Easy scoring
  • Not very useful for shy, withdrawn students
Self-Report Techniques
  • Administration is flexible
  • Interviewees' behavior can be observed, which supplement their content
  • Sharing information in addition to collecting it
  • Extremely time-consuming
  • Information gained is not standard
  • Self-report inventory and questionnaires are common substitutes for personal interviews
